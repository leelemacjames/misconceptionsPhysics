Field type Gravitational field

“Heavier objects fall faster.”
SL
Why: Everyday experience includes air resistance: paper vs stone. Students generalise “more weight = more pull” without separating force from acceleration.
Correct: In the same gravitational field, all masses accelerate at g if drag is negligible. Force is larger on heavier objects, but so is inertia, so a = F/m stays the same.
Exam-style: Two spheres (0.2 kg and 2.0 kg) are dropped from the same height in a vacuum. (a) Compare their accelerations. (b) Compare the time to hit the ground.
Hints: (a) both accelerate at g. (b) same time (independent of mass) if released together from same height with same initial velocity.
“There is no gravity in orbit; astronauts float because gravity is gone.”
SL
Why: “Weightless” is heard as “gravity-free.” Also, pictures show floating without showing the free-fall motion.
Correct: Gravity is still strong in low Earth orbit. Astronauts and spacecraft are in continuous free fall; everything accelerates together so the normal contact force becomes ~0 (apparent weightlessness).
Exam-style: A satellite orbits Earth at constant speed. Explain why the astronauts inside feel weightless, even though Earth’s gravity acts on them.
Hints: free fall; gravity provides centripetal acceleration; no normal reaction force from “floor” needed to change motion relative to spacecraft.
“Geostationary satellites have no acceleration because their speed is constant.”
HL
Why: Students equate “constant speed” with “no acceleration,” forgetting acceleration is change in velocity (direction too).
Correct: Uniform circular motion has centripetal acceleration a = v²/r toward the centre. Gravity supplies the centripetal force.
Exam-style: Explain why a satellite moving at constant speed in a circular orbit is accelerating. Include the direction of the acceleration.
Hints: velocity direction changes; acceleration toward centre; centripetal; magnitude v²/r.
“Gravitational potential energy depends only on height: GPE = mgh is always valid.”
HL
Why: mgh is taught early and used everywhere, so it becomes a “default truth,” even outside uniform fields.
Correct: mgh is an approximation for small height changes near Earth where g is ~constant. Generally, U = -GMm/r and ΔU depends on r.
Exam-style: A probe moves from r = 2R to r = 3R from a planet’s centre. Derive an expression for the change in gravitational potential energy in terms of GMm and R.
Hints: Use U = −GMm/r. ΔU = U₂ − U₁ = −GMm/(3R) − (−GMm/(2R)).

Field type Electric field

“Current is used up in a resistor (so less current leaves than enters).”
SL
Why: Students confuse energy transfer with charge transfer. Bulbs “use electricity,” so they assume charge disappears.
Correct: Charge is conserved. In a series circuit, the current is the same everywhere. Components transfer energy from the electric field to thermal/light energy.
Exam-style: A battery and two resistors are in series. Explain why the current is the same through both resistors but the potential difference may differ.
Hints: series ⇒ same charge flow rate; V drop proportional to R (V=IR); energy per charge differs across components.
“Voltage flows through a circuit.”
SL
Why: Everyday language: “send 12 volts.” Also students want one “thing” to move (like water).
Correct: Voltage is a potential difference: energy transferred per unit charge. Charges flow; voltage is a property of two points in a circuit.
Exam-style: Define potential difference and explain what a voltmeter measures and how it must be connected.
Hints: V = W/Q; measures energy per charge between two points; connected in parallel with component.
“Electric field lines are the paths a charge must follow.”
HL
Why: Diagrams look like “tracks.” Students overlook that motion depends on initial velocity and other forces.
Correct: Field lines show the direction of the force on a positive test charge at a point. Trajectories are found using Newton’s laws (and may cut across lines).
Exam-style: A charged particle enters a uniform electric field with velocity perpendicular to the field. Describe the trajectory and explain why it is not “along a field line.”
Hints: constant force ⇒ constant acceleration; velocity has two components ⇒ parabola (projectile-like). Field lines show force direction only.
“A battery provides a constant current (whatever the circuit).”
SL
Why: Students learn “a 1.5 V battery” and assume it “pushes the same flow” regardless of resistance.
Correct: An ideal battery provides (approximately) constant emf (potential difference). The current depends on circuit resistance: I = V/R (plus internal resistance in real cells).
Exam-style: Two different resistors are connected one at a time to the same cell. Explain how and why the current changes.
Hints: I = V/R; larger R ⇒ smaller I; mention internal resistance if appropriate (terminal p.d. drops at higher I).
“Electric potential is the same thing as electric potential energy.”
HL
Why: The words are similar and both involve “potential.” Students miss the per-unit-charge idea.
Correct: Electric potential V is potential energy per unit charge: V = U/q. Potential energy depends on the charge value; potential does not.
Exam-style: A +2.0 μC charge moves between two points with potential difference 12 V. Calculate the change in electric potential energy and state whether it increases or decreases for motion from high to low potential.
Hints: ΔU = qΔV. From high→low, ΔV is negative, so ΔU negative for positive q (energy decreases).

Field type Magnetic field

“Magnets attract all metals.”
SL
Why: Many classroom “metals” are steel, so the pattern seems universal.
Correct: Strong attraction occurs mainly for ferromagnetic materials (iron, cobalt, nickel and some alloys). Aluminium/copper are not strongly attracted.
Exam-style: Explain why a magnet strongly attracts a steel paperclip but not an aluminium one, even though both are metals.
Hints: ferromagnetism/domains in steel align; aluminium is not ferromagnetic (only weak paramagnetism).
“A stationary charge in a magnetic field experiences a force.”
HL
Why: Students treat magnetic fields like electric fields (force exists “just because field exists”).
Correct: Magnetic force is velocity-dependent: F = qvB sinθ. If v = 0, then F = 0.
Exam-style: A proton enters a uniform magnetic field. Describe how the path changes if (i) its speed doubles, (ii) the entry angle changes from 90° to 30°.
Hints: radius r = mv/(qB); doubling v doubles r; only perpendicular component causes circular motion → helical/greater pitch at smaller angle.
“Induction happens because the magnet ‘gives electrons a push’ even when nothing changes.”
SL
Why: Moving a magnet causes a current, so students attach causality to “magnet near coil” instead of “changing flux.”
Correct: Induced emf occurs when magnetic flux changes: ε = −dΦ/dt. A stationary magnet relative to a coil gives no induced emf (in that frame).
Exam-style: A bar magnet is held still inside a coil connected to a galvanometer. Predict the reading. Then predict what happens when the magnet is pulled out quickly.
Hints: still → no change in flux → zero. Pulled out → flux decreases → transient emf/current; direction given by Lenz’s law.
“Lenz’s law says the induced current always opposes the magnet’s motion.”
HL
Why: “Opposes” gets shortened to “opposes motion.” But it’s the change in flux that’s opposed, not motion itself.
Correct: The induced current produces a magnetic field that opposes the change in flux. Depending on geometry, that can oppose or reinforce aspects of motion.
Exam-style: A coil moves into a region of uniform magnetic field. Use Lenz’s law to determine the direction of the induced current and whether the magnetic force on the coil opposes its entry.
Hints: flux increases; induced field opposes increase; current direction from right-hand rule; magnetic force opposes motion (energy conservation).
“Magnetic field lines start at the North pole and end at the South pole (like electric fields).”
SL
Why: Students transfer the “source/sink” idea from electric charges onto magnets.
Correct: Magnetic field lines are continuous closed loops. Outside a magnet they go N→S, but inside they return S→N.
Exam-style: Sketch the magnetic field pattern around a bar magnet and explain why the field lines must form closed loops.
Hints: no isolated poles observed; ∇·B = 0 idea (qualitative); lines return through magnet.

Field type Nuclear field

“Mass is conserved in nuclear reactions.”
SL
Why: Earlier chemistry reinforces “mass conservation.” Nuclear physics breaks that intuition: energy and mass exchange.
Correct: Total energy is conserved. A mass defect can appear, with released energy E = mc² (binding energy differences).
Exam-style: The mass of reactants in a fission reaction exceeds the mass of products by 0.20 u. Calculate the energy released in MeV.
Hints: 1 u ≈ 931.5 MeV/c² ⇒ E ≈ (0.20)(931.5) MeV.
“Half-life means exactly half the nuclei decay after each half-life.”
SL
Why: “Half” sounds deterministic. Students expect clockwork behaviour, not probability.
Correct: Decay is random for an individual nucleus. Half-life is a statistical property of a large sample; the expected number halves over each half-life.
Exam-style: A sample contains 8.0×10⁸ nuclei with half-life 6.0 h. Estimate the number remaining after 18 h. Explain why the actual number may differ.
Hints: 18 h = 3 half-lives ⇒ N = N0/2³. Random nature ⇒ statistical fluctuations.
“Fusion always releases more energy than fission.”
HL
Why: Media framing: “fusion is powerful.” Students don’t connect energy release to the binding energy per nucleon curve.
Correct: Energy is released when products have higher binding energy per nucleon. Light nuclei fuse; heavy nuclei fission to move toward the iron peak.
Exam-style: Use the binding energy per nucleon curve to explain why fusion of light nuclei and fission of heavy nuclei can both release energy.
Hints: products closer to peak (Fe/Ni region) ⇒ higher BE/A ⇒ energy released equals increase in total binding energy.
“Alpha, beta, gamma are ‘different strengths’ of the same radiation.”
SL
Why: Students interpret “more penetrating” as “stronger version.” They miss that the particles are different.
Correct: α is a helium nucleus, β is an electron/positron, γ is a photon. They differ in charge/mass → different ionising/penetrating power.
Exam-style: A source emits α and γ. Describe suitable shielding and justify using ionising and penetrating power.
Hints: α stopped by paper/skin; γ needs dense material (lead/concrete). α strongly ionising, weakly penetrating; γ opposite.
“Binding energy is the energy that binds nucleons, so a more stable nucleus has ‘less’ binding energy.”
HL
Why: “Bound” sounds like “energy stored inside,” so students assume more stable means “needs less energy.”
Correct: Binding energy is the energy required to separate the nucleus into free nucleons. More stable nuclei have greater binding energy (especially per nucleon).
Exam-style: Explain why a nucleus with larger binding energy per nucleon is more stable. Use the idea of energy required for separation.
Hints: need more energy per nucleon to remove; lower energy state; harder to disrupt; relates to mass defect.

Field type Particle physics

“Antimatter is just ‘negative matter’.”
SL
Why: The anti-electron (positron) has positive charge, so students reduce antimatter to “sign flip.”
Correct: Antiparticles have the same mass but opposite charge and other quantum numbers. They can annihilate with their particle partners to produce photons.
Exam-style: An electron and a positron annihilate at rest. State what is produced and explain how momentum is conserved.
Hints: two gamma photons in opposite directions (to conserve momentum) with total energy 2mc².
“Neutrinos are ‘massless so they don’t matter’.”
SL
Why: Neutrinos rarely interact, so they feel “irrelevant” and are described as “almost nothing.”
Correct: Neutrinos interact weakly, so they penetrate matter easily, but they carry energy and momentum and are essential in beta decay and astrophysics.
Exam-style: In beta-minus decay, explain why an antineutrino is emitted (use conservation laws).
Hints: conserves energy, momentum, and lepton number (and angular momentum qualitatively).
“Quarks can be observed directly if your detector is good enough.”
HL
Why: Students think “smaller microscope” solves everything. They don’t know confinement.
Correct: Quarks are confined; attempting to separate them creates enough energy to form new hadrons (hadronisation). We infer quarks via jets and scattering patterns.
Exam-style: Explain why high-energy collisions produce jets of hadrons rather than isolated quarks.
Hints: confinement; energy in colour field → quark–antiquark pair production; hadronisation into mesons/baryons; jets trace original quark directions.
“Exchange particles are tiny balls flying back and forth like bullets.”
HL
Why: The “particle exchange” metaphor is helpful, but students literalise it as classical collisions.
Correct: In quantum field theory, forces arise from interactions of fields; exchange bosons are a model (often virtual) used to calculate interaction probabilities and momentum transfer.
Exam-style: In the Standard Model, name the exchange particle for electromagnetic interactions and state one property that makes it suitable (e.g., mass/charge).
Hints: photon; massless → long range; neutral; travels at c.
“In particle equations, you can ignore conservation of charge if energy is conserved.”
SL
Why: Students treat “conservation” as one rule (energy) instead of a bundle of independent constraints.
Correct: Particle reactions must satisfy multiple conservation laws (charge, baryon number, lepton number, energy, momentum). Violating any one makes the reaction forbidden.

Topic Simple Harmonic Motion

"The period of oscillation depends on the amplitude."
SL
Why: "Bigger swing = longer journey = more time."
Correct: For ideal SHM, period T depends only on system parameters: T = 2π√(m/k) for spring, T = 2π√(L/g) for pendulum. Larger amplitude increases max velocity proportionally, keeping T constant.
Exam-style: A mass-spring system oscillates with amplitude 5.0 cm. The amplitude is doubled. Explain why the period remains unchanged.
Hints: T = 2π√(m/k); no A in formula; restoring force increases with displacement so max speed scales with amplitude.
Unit Analysis: For T = 2π√(L/g), verify the units. Does √(L/g) give seconds?
Answer: L in m, g in m·s⁻². L/g = m/(m·s⁻²) = s². √s² = s ✓
"The restoring force is constant at all points in the oscillation."
SL
Why: Confusing "constant k" (spring constant) with "constant force."
Correct: SHM defining feature: restoring force is proportional to displacement: F = −kx. Maximum force at maximum displacement; zero force at equilibrium.
Exam-style: Sketch a graph of restoring force vs displacement for SHM. Explain why it's a straight line through the origin.
Hints: F = −kx; negative gradient; passes through origin; gradient = −k.
"The heavier a pendulum bob, the shorter its period."
SL
Why: "Heavier things fall faster" intuition from everyday experience.
Correct: T = 2π√(L/g). Mass doesn't appear! Gravitational restoring force and inertia both scale with m, cancelling out.
Exam-style: Derive the period expression for a simple pendulum and explain why mass does not appear.
Hints: Restoring force = mg sin θ ≈ mgθ; acceleration a = −gx/L; compare with a = −ω²x.
Unit Analysis: A student writes T = 2π√(g/L). Use units to show this must be wrong.
Answer: g/L = (m·s⁻²)/m = s⁻². √s⁻² = s⁻¹ (frequency!), not s. Should be √(L/g).
"All pendulum motion is perfect SHM, for any initial angle."
HL
Why: Textbooks say "pendulum = SHM" without emphasising small-angle approximation.
Correct: True SHM requires F ∝ x. For pendulum, F ∝ sin θ. Only for small θ (<10°) is sin θ ≈ θ, giving approximate SHM.
Exam-style: Explain why a pendulum released from 60° does not exhibit true SHM.
Hints: sin 60° ≠ 60° (rad); restoring force not ∝ displacement; period depends on amplitude.
"Harmonic oscillators go forever (perpetual motion)."
SL
Why: SHM equations have no damping term; "ideal" taken as "real."
Correct: Real oscillators experience damping. Energy converts to thermal energy. Ideal SHM is a model; real oscillations decay.
Exam-style: Sketch an x-t graph for light damping. How does amplitude change?
Hints: Exponential decay envelope; amplitude decreases; period approximately constant.
"A pendulum accelerates through the lowest point of its swing."
SL
Why: "It's moving fastest there, so it must be accelerating."
Correct: At lowest point: displacement = 0, restoring force = 0, tangential acceleration = 0. Max speed but only centripetal acceleration (toward pivot).
Exam-style: State the direction and magnitude of (a) velocity (b) acceleration at the lowest point.
Hints: (a) horizontal, max (b) vertical/upward (centripetal), v²/L; tangential = 0.
"Amplitude is measured peak-to-peak."
SL
Why: Oscilloscope settings often show peak-to-peak.
Correct: Amplitude A = max displacement from equilibrium (centre to peak). Peak-to-peak = 2A.
Exam-style: Peak-to-peak displacement is 8.0 cm. State the amplitude and write x = A cos(ωt).
Hints: A = 4.0 cm; x = 0.040 cos(ωt) m.
"Acceleration is zero at the endpoints of pendulum motion."
SL
Why: "It stops there, so no motion, so no acceleration."
Correct: At max displacement: velocity = 0 but acceleration is maximum! a = −ω²x, so max |a| when max |x|.
Exam-style: Sketch x, v, a vs t for SHM. Where is v = 0? What's a there?
Hints: v = 0 at x = ±A; acceleration = ∓ω²A (max magnitude).

Topic Introduction to Waves

"Waves transport matter."
SL
Why: Ocean waves push things to shore; "movement" suggests matter moves with wave.
Correct: Waves transport energy, not matter. Medium particles oscillate about fixed positions. A cork bobs up and down but doesn't travel with the wave.
Exam-style: Describe motion of a point on a rope as a wave passes. What is transferred?
Hints: Point oscillates ⊥ to wave direction; energy transferred; no net matter displacement.
"There must be a medium for a wave to travel through."
SL
Why: Most familiar waves (sound, water) need media.
Correct: Mechanical waves need media. EM waves don't – they're self-propagating E and B field oscillations that travel through vacuum.
Exam-style: Why can starlight reach Earth through vacuum but sound cannot?
Hints: Light is EM (no medium needed); sound is mechanical (needs particles).
"Waves do not have energy."
SL
Why: Energy associated with "things." Waves seem like "patterns."
Correct: Waves carry energy: sound vibrates eardrums; light warms surfaces; tsunamis are destructive.
Exam-style: A 5.0 mW laser runs for 2.0 min. Calculate energy delivered.
Hints: E = Pt = 5.0×10⁻³ × 120 = 0.60 J.
Unit Analysis: Intensity I = P/A. What are SI units of intensity?
Answer: W/m² = W·m⁻² ✓
"Frequency is connected to loudness."
SL
Why: High-pitched sounds feel more piercing.
Correct: Frequency → pitch; amplitude → loudness (I ∝ A²). A bass note can be louder than a high note.
Exam-style: Same frequency, A doubles amplitude of B. Compare pitch and loudness.
Hints: Same pitch; A is louder (I ∝ A², so 4× intensity).
"Big waves travel faster than small waves in the same medium."
SL
Why: Bigger = more powerful, so should travel faster.
Correct: Wave speed depends on medium properties, not amplitude. Sound in air: all amplitudes travel at ~343 m/s.
Exam-style: Two speakers, different loudness, same f. Why do sounds arrive simultaneously?
Hints: Speed set by medium; amplitude affects energy, not speed.
Unit Analysis: v = √(γP/ρ). Verify this gives m/s.
Answer: P/ρ = (kg·m⁻¹·s⁻²)/(kg·m⁻³) = m²·s⁻². √ = m·s⁻¹ ✓
"Different colours of light are different types of waves."
SL
Why: Red and blue look completely different.
Correct: All visible light is EM radiation – same type. Colours differ only in frequency/wavelength.
Exam-style: Red: 700 nm, blue: 450 nm. Calculate frequencies. What determines colour?
Hints: f = c/λ; red ≈ 4.3×10¹⁴ Hz, blue ≈ 6.7×10¹⁴ Hz; frequency → colour.
"Pitch is related to intensity."
SL
Why: "High" pitch and "high" volume both use "high."
Correct: Pitch ↔ frequency; loudness ↔ intensity. They're independent.
Exam-style: Tuning fork 256 Hz and whistle 2048 Hz at same intensity. Compare pitch and loudness.
Hints: Same loudness; whistle higher pitch.

Topic Wave Nature of Light

"Light is a particle" / "Light is a mixture of particles and waves."
SL
Why: Wave-particle duality taught as "both" interpreted as literal mixture.
Correct: Light exhibits wave behaviour (interference) and particle behaviour (photoelectric). It's a quantum entity we model differently depending on the experiment.
Exam-style: Describe one experiment showing wave nature and one showing particle nature of light.
Hints: Wave: double-slit interference. Particle: photoelectric effect (threshold f, instant emission).
"Light waves and radio waves are not the same thing."
SL
Why: We see light but not radio. Different names suggest different things.
Correct: Both are EM radiation – oscillating E and B fields at c. Only differ in f/λ. EM spectrum is continuous.
Exam-style: Three properties same for light and radio waves. One that differs.
Hints: Same: speed c, transverse, can polarise. Different: frequency, wavelength, photon energy.
"The speed of light never changes."
SL
Why: "c is constant" becomes "light always at c."
Correct: c in vacuum ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s. In media: v = c/n. In glass (n ≈ 1.5), v ≈ 2×10⁸ m/s.
Exam-style: Light enters glass n = 1.52. Calculate speed. What happens to wavelength?
Hints: v = c/n ≈ 1.97×10⁸ m/s; λ decreases (f constant).
Unit Analysis: n = c/v. Show n is dimensionless.
Answer: (m·s⁻¹)/(m·s⁻¹) = 1 (dimensionless) ✓
"Rays and wavefronts are the same thing."
SL
Why: Both are "lines on light diagrams."
Correct: Rays show direction of energy propagation (⊥ to wavefronts). Wavefronts connect points of equal phase.
Exam-style: Draw wavefronts and rays for plane wave and point source. State their relationship.
Hints: Rays always ⊥ to wavefronts. Plane: parallel lines/arrows. Point: circles/radial arrows.
"In refraction, the frequency (colour) of light changes."
SL
Why: Prisms make rainbows, so refraction "changes" colours.
Correct: Frequency set by source, unchanged at boundaries. v and λ change. Dispersion occurs because n depends on f.
Exam-style: Light f = 5×10¹⁴ Hz enters water n = 1.33. Find λ in water. Why is f unchanged?
Hints: v = c/n; λ = v/f ≈ 4.5×10⁻⁷ m; boundary continuity requires same f.
"Refraction is the 'bending' of waves."
SL
Why: "Bend" in textbooks implies curves.
Correct: Refraction = direction change at boundary due to speed change. Wave travels straight in each medium. "Bend" describes interface angle change.
Exam-style: Use Huygens' principle to explain why waves bend toward normal when entering slower medium.
Hints: Secondary wavelets smaller in slow medium → tilted wavefront → direction toward normal.
"Double-slit shows crests and troughs – bright where crests meet."
HL
Why: Water wave analogies show crests as high points.
Correct: Bright fringes where waves arrive in phase (path diff = nλ). Could be crest+crest OR trough+trough. Constructive superposition.
Exam-style: First bright fringe has path difference λ. Explain why it's bright.
Hints: Path diff λ → phase diff 2π (=0); waves in phase; constructive; amplitude doubles.
Unit Analysis: s = λD/d. If λ in nm, D in m, d in mm, how to get s in m?
Answer: Convert: λ×10⁻⁹, d×10⁻³. Then (m×m)/m = m ✓
"Adding all colours of light yields black."
SL
Why: Mixing paints (subtractive) gives dark colours.
Correct: Additive mixing: all visible frequencies = white. Subtractive (pigments) absorbs light → darker. Light adds; pigments subtract.
Exam-style: Why does R+G+B light = white, but R+G+B paint = dark?
Hints: Light additive (more→white). Paint subtractive (each absorbs→less light→dark).

Field Gravitational field

"Heavier objects fall faster."
SL
Why: Air resistance (paper vs stone). "More weight = more pull" without separating F from a.
Correct: All masses accelerate at g (no drag). F larger but so is inertia, so a = F/m stays same.
Exam: 0.2 kg and 2.0 kg spheres dropped in vacuum. Compare (a) accelerations (b) times to fall.
Hints: Both g; same time.
"No gravity in orbit – astronauts float because gravity is gone."
SL
Why: "Weightless" = "gravity-free."
Correct: Gravity strong in LEO. Continuous free fall; everything accelerates together → apparent weightlessness.
Exam: Explain why astronauts feel weightless despite gravity.
Hints: Free fall; g provides centripetal a; no normal force needed.
"Geostationary satellites have no acceleration – constant speed."
HL
Why: Constant speed = no acceleration.
Correct: Circular motion has centripetal a = v²/r toward centre. Direction changes.
Exam: Explain why constant-speed orbit involves acceleration.
Hints: Velocity direction changes; a toward centre; v²/r.
"GPE = mgh is always valid."
HL
Why: mgh used everywhere, becomes "default."
Correct: mgh approximation for small h where g ≈ constant. Generally U = −GMm/r.
Exam: Probe moves r = 2R to 3R. Find ΔU in terms of GMm/R.
Hints: ΔU = −GMm/3R − (−GMm/2R) = GMm/6R.
Unit: U = −GMm/r. Verify gives J.
Answer: (N·m²·kg⁻²)(kg)(kg)/m = N·m = J ✓

Field Electric field

"A moving charge always follows a field line."
SL
Why: Field lines look like tracks.
Correct: Lines show force direction. With initial velocity not along line → curved trajectory (Newton's laws).
Exam: Electron enters uniform E-field perpendicular to lines. Describe motion.
Hints: Parabolic path; constant force; like projectile.
"If not on a field line, no force."
SL
Why: Diagrams show discrete lines.
Correct: Field everywhere continuous. Lines are visualization; density shows strength. F = qE at any point.
Exam: Why does a charge between drawn field lines still feel a force?
Hints: Field continuous; lines representative; more lines = stronger.
"Field lines are real physical things."
SL
Why: Iron filings look like lines.
Correct: Lines are Faraday's visualization tool. Field is continuous vector quantity.
Exam: State two conventions for drawing E-field lines and their meaning.
Hints: + to −; tangent = direction; density = magnitude; don't cross.
"Electric field and force are the same thing."
SL
Why: Both vectors involving charges.
Correct: E = F/q (force per unit charge). Field exists without test charge. F = qE depends on q.
Exam: E = 500 N/C. Find force on (a) +2 μC (b) −3 μC.
Hints: F = qE; (a) 1 mN along E; (b) 1.5 mN opposite E.
"Coulomb's law applies to any charge distribution."
HL
Why: Apply F = kq₁q₂/r² everywhere.
Correct: Simple form for point charges. Extended distributions need integration or Gauss's law.
Exam: Why can uniformly charged spheres be treated as point charges? When does this fail?
Hints: Shell theorem; fails if overlap, non-uniform, or polarisation.
Unit: What are units of k in F = kq₁q₂/r²?
Answer: N·m²·C⁻² ✓
"Electric potential = electric potential energy."
HL
Why: Similar words.
Correct: V = U/q (energy per unit charge). U depends on q; V does not.
Exam: +2 μC moves through 12 V. Find ΔU. Does it increase or decrease high→low?
Hints: ΔU = qΔV = 24 μJ; high→low: ΔV neg → ΔU neg (decreases).
"A charged body has only one type of charge."
SL
Why: "The rod is positive" sounds like only + present.
Correct: All matter has both p and e. "Positive" = net excess/deficit. Both types always present.
Exam: Explain at atomic level how glass becomes positive by rubbing silk.
Hints: Electrons transfer to silk; glass loses e⁻; still has protons; net +.

Field Magnetic field

"North/south poles are like positive/negative charges."
SL
Why: Opposites attract in both; similar diagrams.
Correct: Magnetic poles always in pairs (dipoles). Electric charges can be isolated. No magnetic monopoles observed.
Exam: Why does cutting a magnet produce two complete magnets, but charges can be separated?
Hints: Atomic dipoles; each piece still has N and S; no monopoles.
"Magnetic field lines start at N and end at S."
SL
Why: Diagrams show lines "leaving" N, "entering" S.
Correct: Magnetic lines are closed loops. Outside: N→S. Inside magnet: S→N. ∇·B = 0.
Exam: Sketch field inside and outside bar magnet. Why must lines form closed loops?
Hints: Lines continue through magnet S→N; no monopoles.
"Charges at rest feel magnetic forces."
HL
Why: Magnets attract things not visibly moving.
Correct: F = qvB sin θ. If v = 0, F = 0. Magnetic force requires motion.
Exam: Electron at rest in B-field. Describe motion. Compare to E-field.
Hints: B: no force, stays at rest. E: accelerates along field.
"Magnets attract all metals."
SL
Why: Most classroom metals are steel.
Correct: Strong attraction only for ferromagnetic materials (Fe, Co, Ni). Aluminium/copper not strongly attracted.

"Induction happens when magnet is near coil (even stationary)."
SL
Why: Moving magnet → current, so magnet near coil should work.
Correct: Induced emf requires changing flux: ε = −dΦ/dt. Stationary magnet → no flux change → no emf.
Exam: Magnet held still in coil. Galvanometer reading? Then pulled out quickly?
Hints: Still: zero. Pulled out: transient current (Lenz's law).
"Lenz's law: induced current opposes magnet's motion."
HL
Why: "Opposes" shortened to "opposes motion."
Correct: Induced current produces B-field that opposes change in flux, not motion itself.
Exam: Coil moves into uniform B-field. Use Lenz's law to find current direction.
Hints: Flux increases; induced B opposes increase; current from RHR.
Unit: Flux Φ = BA. What are units of Φ?
Answer: T·m² = Wb (weber). Also Wb = V·s ✓
"Earth's geographic north is a magnetic north pole."
SL
Why: Both called "north."
Correct: Compass N-pole points to geographic N because it's attracted there → geographic N is magnetic S!
Exam: Why does compass N point to geographic N? What's the magnetic polarity there?
Hints: Opposite poles attract; geographic N = magnetic S.

Field Nuclear field

"Mass is conserved in nuclear reactions."
SL
Why: Chemistry teaches mass conservation.
Correct: Total energy conserved. Mass defect → energy: E = mc².
Exam: Mass defect 0.20 u in fission. Calculate energy in MeV.
Hints: 1 u ≈ 931.5 MeV/c²; E ≈ 186 MeV.
"Half-life means exactly half decay each time."
SL
Why: "Half" sounds deterministic.
Correct: Decay is random. Half-life is statistical for large samples; expected number halves.
Exam: 8×10⁸ nuclei, t½ = 6 h. Estimate N after 18 h. Why might actual differ?
Hints: 3 half-lives → N/8 = 10⁸; statistical fluctuations.
"α, β, γ are different strengths of same radiation."
SL
Why: "More penetrating" = "stronger."
Correct: α = He nucleus, β = electron/positron, γ = photon. Different particles entirely.
Exam: Source emits α and γ. Describe suitable shielding.
Hints: α: paper/skin; γ: lead/concrete.
"Binding energy: more stable = less BE."
HL
Why: "Bound" sounds like energy stored inside.
Correct: BE = energy to separate nucleus. More stable = greater BE (per nucleon).
Exam: Why is larger BE/A = more stable?
Hints: More energy needed to remove nucleon; lower energy state.

Field Particle physics

"Antimatter is just 'negative matter'."
SL
Why: Positron has + charge, so "opposite sign."
Correct: Same mass, opposite charge and quantum numbers. Annihilate with partners → photons.
Exam: e⁻ and e⁺ annihilate at rest. What's produced? How is momentum conserved?
Hints: Two γ photons opposite directions; E = 2mc².
"Quarks can be observed directly."
HL
Why: "Better detector" solves everything.
Correct: Quarks confined. Separation creates energy → new hadrons (hadronisation). Inferred via jets.
Exam: Why do high-energy collisions produce jets, not isolated quarks?
Hints: Confinement; energy → q-qbar pairs; hadronisation.
"Can ignore charge conservation if energy conserved."
SL
Why: "Conservation" = one rule (energy).
Correct: Must conserve charge, baryon #, lepton #, energy, momentum. Violate any → forbidden.
Exam: Is p → n + e⁺ possible? Use two conservation laws.
Hints: Charge OK (+1→0+1); lepton # fails (0→0+(−1)).
